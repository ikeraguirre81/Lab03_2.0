From this metrics, we see that the Logistic Regression is predicting pretty well both the positives and negatives. There are very high false positive and true positives rates (from normalized confusion matrix). Also, we observe a really high AUC of 0.98

Hence, we have really high levels of precision and recall with our Logistic Regression. In our case, we will be focusing on the precision metric because our positives represent the case when the loan is fully paid, and the negatives represent otherwise. So, theoretically, our model would be good in accounting for those people who are categorized as clients that will pay but end up entering in default. That error is the one we need to minimize, because conceding a loan which wont be paid could very very costly for company.

As there are high levels of both precision and recall, we can see a rectangular trade-off between the two in the pr-curve now presented:

Cumulative Gains Curve¶

We can also assess the cumulative gains curve and the lift curve. These are good measures of the perfomance of our model.

On the one hand, the Cumulative Gains Curve shows the percentage of targets reached when considering a certain percentage of the population with the highest probability to be target according to the model. In our case, at the 20% point of the horizontal axis, the 80% observations with the highest probability are located. On the vertical axis, the curve indicates which percentage of all targets is included in this curve.

For both Random Forest and XGBoost models, we observe again high levels of accuracy and of AUC. They also behave similarly to the logistic regression in the other metrics, which is quite good. Nevertheless, this fantastic perfomance raises some concerns of how well our models are really representing the reality, and therefore how well will these predict the defaults of loans. We will approach these concerns below.

When considering which model would fit better with this classification problem, we would definetly have to choose between either Random Forest or XGBoost, both over a 96% of accuracy, which is quite high.

There were also very good outcomes when predicting with the other two models, placing correctly the majority of the positive cases, which are the ones we say the are going to pay, and end up paying indeed. However, the Logistic Regression was way better for different reasons:

  1. There was an obvious accuracy advantage (95% for LR, 79% for SVM)
  1. The Support Vector Machine model clearly misplaced a high volume of default loans (17% of predicted defaults) which, even though it compensates with a very low error classifying the fully paid ones (less than 1%), is very dangerous if we take into account that in the banking industry is way more severe to concede a future default loan than not condecing a loan that will probably be paid.

For that reason, this would be our order of preference when choosing a model:

  1. XGBoost (98% accuracy in test set, AUC = 1.00)
  2. Random Forest (96% accuracy in test set)
  3. Logistic Regression
  4. Support Vector Machine

As we have seen above, our models perform really well and have high levels on the AUC metric as well as in others. However, we fear that these results are biased due to the selection of the predictor variables. It is possible that som of the variables that have been chosen to predict the default, are actually highly correlated with it, and thus, irrelevant for our model. Presumably, these variables reflect data that can only be seen in the future, once the loan is already conceeded but not just right before clearing it.

To try and clarify this, we will proceed by droping from our dataset some variables that we believe are of this type. Then, we will train a model and obtain the AUC metric in order to compare with the first models, which include all variables. We cannot be sure as the clients that are now applying for a loan in LC, could have applied and being conceeded in other institutions before. And therefore, the variables could be referring to previous loans of the clients. To shed some light on this we can visualize the importance of some of the features that we consider should be removed from our model: last_pymnt_amnt and out_prncp_inv

First we remove those variables.
